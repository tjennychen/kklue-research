# KKLue Social Media Strategy: Stress-Test Debate Framework
## Purpose, Format, and Personas

**Version 1.0 | March 2026**
**Step A of 3 — Structure only. Full simulation pending Brutalist review.**

---

## Purpose

This document stages a structured peer-to-peer debate to stress-test KKLue's social media marketing strategy before Year 1 execution begins.

The debate brings together 7 distinct voices — three from KKLue's potential customer and creator base, four from the business and operational world surrounding the brand. Each persona holds genuine stakes and genuine skepticism. None of them agree on everything. That's the point.

The goal is not consensus. The goal is to surface real tensions before they surface in the market, expose assumptions that haven't been stress-tested, and arrive at a social media strategy that can be defended from every angle — customer, investor, competitor, founder, and ops.

A strategy that survives this room is a strategy worth executing.

**What this is not:** a marketing persona deck. These are not archetypes designed to make the strategy look good. They're designed to make it hard.

## The 7 Personas

### INFLUENCER GROUP
*These three represent KKLue's potential customer and creator base. They are the people KKLue needs to earn.*

### 1. Jade Chen — The Asian-American Tastemaker

**Platform:** 220K Instagram / 85K TikTok
**Location:** New York City
**Day job:** UX designer at a tech company

Jade posts in English, occasionally bilingual. Her aesthetic is quiet luxury, intentional minimalism — the kind of feed where every saved post has been sitting in a collection called "Actually want this" for months before it becomes a purchase. She's influential without being loud about it. Her followers trust her recommendations precisely because she doesn't make them often.

She knows the jewelry landscape. She bought from Mejuri early — loved the first piece, her second piece tarnished within six months. She's still annoyed about it in a way that's shaped every jewelry decision since. She knows Kinn. She knows Aurate. She currently wears a single thin 14K chain daily. One thing. Real.

She discovers brands through creators she already trusts, saved posts that resurface when she's ready to buy, and occasionally through Reddit's r/femalefashionadvice when she's doing research. She does not respond to ads. Not as a principle — she's just learned they're never worth it.

Before a $300 purchase, she does homework. She reads all the reviews. She checks the founder's Instagram. She looks for real wear photos — not editorial, not flatlays, actual photos of the piece on someone's wrist after three months of daily use. She wants to see if it's still holding up.

Her hot button: "If the brand story feels constructed, I'm out. I can tell within two seconds of landing on a page."

**The tension she creates for KKLue:** She is the ideal customer profile and the hardest conversion. She's been burned, she's sophisticated, and she's patient — but the framework must not misread what "burned before" means. A burned customer does not become more ready for the next brand. She becomes more suspicious of ALL brands in the same category. Jade's Mejuri tarnish experience is not an opening for KKLue. It's a liability KKLue has to overcome. She will wait six months and do more research rather than buy something she's not sure about. KKLue has to earn her — and that means not a single claim that doesn't hold up, from the first second of contact.

### 2. Amy Liu — The Practical Mom

**Platform:** 32K Instagram / 48K TikTok
**Location:** Suburban Connecticut
**Day job:** Marketing manager

Two kids, ages 7 and 4. Amy is not fashion-forward and she knows it — she shops at Madewell, J.Crew, occasionally Mejuri range when she's treating herself. For her, jewelry isn't fashion. It's self-care. It's the one thing that's just hers in a life that mostly belongs to everyone else.

Two years ago she bought a "gold" necklace from an Instagram brand. It turned green in a month. She was furious — not because of the money, but because she'd felt dumb for trusting it. That experience lives in her like a warning light that doesn't turn off. Now she won't buy anything claiming to be gold without proof.

She discovers brands through TikTok's for-you page, mom creator vlogs, and word of mouth inside her WeChat mom group. She's second-generation Chinese-American, so that group is active and trusted — these are her people, and when one of them says a brand is real, she listens. More than any ad she'll ever see.

Her hot button: "Just tell me exactly what I'm buying. What karat? Made where? Will it survive my life — gym, pool, two kids?"

She responds to proof, not polish. A creator wearing a necklace while doing dishes for six months, showing it still looks new — that's worth more to her than any professional photoshoot. The photoshoot is what burned her last time.

**The tension she creates for KKLue:** She's extremely high-value. Moms buy gifts. Mom networks are referral engines. A mom who loves a brand tells five people. But the bar to get there is real — she needs social proof she actually trusts, from sources she's seen before, about specifics she can verify. Marketing language will not work on Amy. Facts will.

### 3. Xinyi (Vivian) Zhang — The Brand Insider

**Platform:** 18K XHS followers / 9K Instagram
**Location:** Los Angeles (3 years post-HK immigration)
**Day job:** UX researcher at a startup

Vivian already owns three KKLue pieces: the Moment necklace, the Unlock lock, a pair of earrings. She bought them in Hong Kong before she moved. She didn't buy them because they were pretty — she bought them because everyone around her trusted the brand. KKLue in HK isn't just jewelry. It's a brand women give to each other at milestones. That trust was built over years.

She lives primarily on XHS for Chinese content and Instagram for English content. She's embedded in both communities. She understands, more than anyone in this room, what KKLue actually represents to the women who've been wearing it for years — and she's watching very carefully to see how that gets translated for US audiences.

Her hot button: "KKLue is not just jewelry in HK — it's a brand women trust with their actual names. That trust is real. It should transfer here."

Her worry: "I'm afraid that if KKLue tries too hard to look American, they'll lose the thing that makes them special. The moment I see language like 'modern minimalism for the everyday woman,' I'll know it's gone."

**Her role in the debate:** Vivian is the cultural translator — and the most conflicted person in the room. She holds both sides, but the harder question nobody asks her is this: does "authentically HK" land well with the Chinese diaspora in 2026, or does HK carry political baggage that complicates a straightforward pride narrative? Does second-generation Chinese-American identity align with HK identity, or is the relationship more complicated than this strategy assumes? Vivian is the voice of "don't dilute" — but she also has to answer whether the thing she's trying to preserve actually travels the way she thinks it does.

### 4. The Competitor Voice — "Alex"
*(Composite: what a Kinn Studio / Mejuri veteran would say)*

Alex is not a real person. Alex is what five-plus years of US fine jewelry DTC experience sounds like when it's concentrated into one voice — someone who has watched forty-plus jewelry brands try to enter this market, and has seen the pattern of how most of them end.

The tone is not hostile. It's direct. "We respect the product. We've seen this pitch before."

Alex has nothing against KKLue. The product is real. The story is legitimate. But the market doesn't care about legitimate — the market cares about distribution, and distribution in DTC jewelry is won through years of consistent community-building, not through having better materials.

Alex's standing challenges:

- "Does Hong Kong origin actually matter to a woman in Austin or Chicago who has never been to Asia? Be specific. Because 'HK craft tradition' is something your team understands. It's not something she has context for."
- "Mejuri already has 18K solid gold pieces in their catalog. Most customers don't know because Mejuri doesn't lead with it — they lead with price and aesthetics. Your entire differentiator depends on consumer education you don't have budget for. What's your cost to educate 1,000 women on why 18K matters? Run that number before you call it a moat."
- "Your moat is material quality — solid gold versus vermeil. What's the durable differentiator beyond materials? Because the story has to be deeper than 'we use better metal.'"
- "You have social proof in Chinese on a platform that most US buyers have never heard of. That social proof doesn't transfer. You're starting from zero with an audience that doesn't speak the language of your proof."

What Alex would do differently: "Pick one product, one customer, one channel. Not three of each. Go so deep in one place that you own it before you expand."

### 5. The Investor Voice — "Priya"
*(DTC-experienced angel investor; has seen the Mejuri journey up close)*

Priya has backed three DTC jewelry and accessories brands. One won big — Mejuri-adjacent trajectory, exits at multiples she's proud of. One flamed out in Year 2 when CAC grew faster than LTV. One is still finding its footing, two years in, burning cash on awareness with no repeat purchase to show for it.

She knows the failure mode. She's seen it three times. And she's sitting in this room because KKLue's pitch is interesting enough to take seriously — which also means she's going to ask the questions no one else will ask.

What she needs to see before she's interested: CAC by channel (especially paid vs. organic), LTV on a $300 first purchase, repeat purchase rate and time-to-second-purchase, gross margin on solid gold (it's tighter than vermeil brands), and one clear, honest answer to "why will this customer choose KKLue over Mejuri on the day she first finds both?"

Her read on the current market: "DTC jewelry is crowded with genuinely good products. The winners have either a distribution moat — Mejuri built a community before they had a business — or a product moat, which Kinn has through their specific cultural identity angle. I'm still working out which one KKLue has. Product quality alone has never been the moat."

Her hot button: "Year 1 is not about revenue. Year 1 is about proving repeat purchase and organic referral. Those are the only metrics that tell me whether the brand is working or whether you're just buying transactions. Show me those numbers at Month 12 and we'll have a real conversation."

Her core challenge: "The Asian-American beachhead is a smart starting point. But I need a clear, specific thesis for how that community becomes the bridge to broader US appeal — and that thesis has to be more than 'they'll tell their friends.' Who will they tell? What will they say? Why will those people care?"

### 6. Kellyn Zhou — The Founder

Kellyn left a corporate career in Hong Kong at 26 after attending a jewelry exhibition that she's described as the moment she stopped pretending she was on the right path. She didn't just decide to make jewelry — she formally studied jewelry design and appraisal first. She learned the craft before she built the brand.

She started with a bespoke studio. She built from there to a brand with two flagship mall stores in HK's most premium retail locations (IFC in Central, Harbour City in TST) and over 300,000 customers. The brand name comes from the word "clue" — each piece is a clue to who you are. That's not a tagline someone wrote. That's something she actually believes.

In this debate, Kellyn is the gravity. When the conversation drifts toward marketing claims that outrun the product, she pulls it back. When someone proposes a strategy that would require KKLue to perform something it isn't, she says so.

Her hot button: "I don't want us to claim things we haven't earned. The brand's integrity is everything. Once we lose that, we lose the thing that made 300,000 women trust us."

Her instinct going into this: "I want to start with real stories from real customers. Not a channel strategy. The channel strategy should follow the story, not precede it."

Her growth edge: Kellyn is deeply indexed on product truth and craft — which is her strength and, in a US market context, occasionally her blind spot. She understands what makes the jewelry good. She's still learning what makes an American woman with no prior exposure to the brand trust it enough to spend $300. She needs to be asked harder questions about consumer psychology, and she's honest enough to admit when she doesn't know the answer.

What she most wants to understand coming out of this: "Who is the American woman who will love this the way the women in Hong Kong love it? Not her demographics. Her. What does she believe? What has she been through? Why would this matter to her?"

### 7. The Distributor / Ops Voice — "Marcus"

Marcus runs a 3PL operation handling fulfillment for 12 DTC jewelry brands, including two at price points comparable to KKLue. He's seen what happens when a brand goes viral without ops infrastructure to catch the demand. He's also seen what happens when a brand over-invests in ops before validating demand. He's not here to kill the strategy — he's here to make sure it survives contact with the real world.

His concerns are logistical and specific:

The made-to-order model is actually protective in some ways — no dead inventory, no markdown pressure, no cash tied up in a warehouse. But it means capacity planning has to happen before the demand spike, not after. And a 10-day production lead time plus international shipping from Hong Kong equals 18-21 days to a US customer's door. In a market where Amazon has trained buyers to expect two-day delivery and Mejuri ships from a US warehouse, 18-21 days is not a small ask. It's a conversion killer for last-minute gift occasions unless it's framed as a feature — made for you, takes time, worth the wait — not buried in the FAQ.

His standing challenge: "What happens when a TikTok video goes to a million views on a Friday? What's your production capacity ceiling at KKLue's workshops? What's the call you make at 500 orders in a week versus 50? I need to know that before you run any influencer campaign, because the ops failure during the viral moment is the one that's hardest to recover from."

His recommendation: build a small inventory buffer of the three or four most-ordered letter combinations before any major campaign push. It doesn't need to be large. It just needs to exist so that the brand's first impression on a first-time customer isn't a three-week wait when they were expecting less than two.

## The 5-Round Debate Structure

*This section shows structure and opening positions only. The full simulation (Step C) will expand each position into real dialogue, interruptions, and cross-examination. What's shown here is each persona's opening stance — the place they start from before the room pushes back.*

---

### Round 1: "What is the one social media channel we commit to in Year 1?"

**Why this question:** Everyone will answer differently, and every different answer reveals a hidden assumption about who the target customer actually is and how she finds brands she trusts. This is the fastest way to surface whether the team actually agrees on the customer — before they've explicitly argued about her.

**Opening positions:**

**Jade:** Instagram, without question. But not brand Instagram — creator Instagram. Seed the right five to eight accounts with real product and let them tell the story in their own voice. A brand account with 3K followers doesn't move me. A creator I already trust wearing something for three months and still posting about it does.

**Amy:** TikTok. That's where I find things I didn't know I needed. But it has to be content that feels like a real person talking, not a brand ad. Show me someone wearing it during her actual life. Not a launch shoot. Real life.

**Vivian:** You can't ignore XHS. That's where the early adopters — the diaspora women who already have context for KKLue — are going to find this and trust it. XHS first, use that proof to build English-language credibility. Don't abandon the community that's already primed to believe you.

**Alex:** Pick one and go deep enough that you actually own a moment on it before you touch the next one. The mistake everyone makes is spreading thin across three channels and building no meaningful presence anywhere. Depth over breadth. Year 1 is not the time to be on every platform.

**Priya:** The channel question is downstream of the customer question. Who are you trying to reach in Year 1 — and where does she actually spend time in the moment she's ready to spend $300 on jewelry? Answer that first. Then the channel answer is obvious.

**Kellyn:** I want to start with the women who already know us. Our XHS community, the diaspora women who've heard of KKLue, women who've been gifted a piece. Let them be the first voice. Channel strategy built on strangers feels fragile. Built on people who already trust the brand — that has a foundation.

**Marcus:** Whichever channel you choose, build your ops infrastructure to handle a demand spike from that channel before you turn it on. TikTok in particular can move very fast. I've seen brands hit a viral moment they weren't ready for and lose customers they can never get back.

---

### Round 2: "Who is the target customer — describe her in one sentence. No demographics allowed. Then: everyone must agree on ONE sentence."

**Why this question:** "Asian-American women, 25-40, US market" is a targeting spec, not a customer understanding. This round forces everyone to articulate her by values, psychology, and what she believes — not by where she lives or how old she is. The second half of the round is harder: the room must converge on ONE agreed sentence. If they can't, Alex gets to say: "This sentence describes 3 million women who aren't buying from you. Which 500 are you actually talking to?" No parallel presentations allowed in the simulation. Force the room to fight toward agreement.

**Opening positions (before forced convergence):**

**Jade:** She's done buying things that lie to her about what they are, and she's willing to pay more and wait longer for something that doesn't.

**Amy:** She wants one thing in her life that's just hers — beautiful and real and completely out of her to-do list — and she needs someone she trusts to tell her this one is worth it.

**Vivian:** She's a woman who gives jewelry to mark the real moments — her own name, her kid's initial, the word she's trying to become — and she needs a brand that takes that seriously, not one that makes it into a cute campaign.

**Alex:** She's an existing Mejuri or Kinn customer who's starting to wonder if what she bought is actually holding up — and she's open to something better if the proof is real. *(Then:)* Now — all seven of you pick one sentence. If you can't, you don't have a customer. You have a wish.

**Priya:** She's the customer who bought once from a DTC brand she trusted, had a good enough experience that she told two people about it, and is now in market for a second piece from that same brand or a brand exactly like it. That repeat behavior is the one KKLue needs to find and own.

**Kellyn:** She's a woman who knows her jewelry should mean something, but hasn't found a brand that treats that seriously without charging Tiffany prices for it.

**Marcus:** I'll let you handle the customer — I just want to know how she expects her package to arrive and what happens if it's late.

**Note for simulation:** This round must end with the room attempting (and possibly failing) to agree on one sentence. The fight toward convergence is the content. The agreed or disagreed sentence is the output.

---

### Round 3: "What's KKLue's real differentiator — beyond materials?"

**Why this question:** The material story (solid 18K gold at demi-fine prices) is genuinely compelling and genuinely true. But "we use better metal" is a feature, not a brand. This round forces the deeper question: what does KKLue have that can't be replicated by a competitor with more brand awareness, more US reviews, and a bigger influencer budget? If the answer is only "18K gold," the brand is vulnerable to any well-resourced incumbent that decides to compete on the same claim. Note: competition copying is not a Year 1 concern — if a big brand copies KKLue, that's a success problem. The real question is what the brand stands for beyond the material.

**Opening positions:**

**Jade:** The material story gets me to the door. The founder story keeps me there. Kellyn's background is real in a way I can verify — she studied the craft, she didn't just start a brand. That's not replicable. But it only works if you lead with it, not bury it.

**Amy:** Honestly? If Mejuri added solid gold and was totally transparent about it, I'd probably trust Mejuri more because I already know the name. KKLue's differentiator has to be the proof of trust that's already been built — the 300,000 customers in HK, the wear-it-in-the-shower content, the women posting their own pieces a year later. That proof is real and it can't be faked retroactively.

**Vivian:** Mejuri can copy the material. They cannot copy the personalization model at KKLue's level — up to nine letters, Brand Font, made to order. And they absolutely cannot copy the cultural specificity. KKLue's name stories — women putting their own names, their kids' names, the name of the person they lost — that's not a product category. That's a community with a ritual. That takes years.

**Alex:** The durable differentiator is the one thing no US brand structurally has: genuine HK craft provenance. Not as a marketing claim — as something a customer can verify. Kellyn studied jewelry design formally. The stores are in IFC and Harbour City. These are facts, not taglines. The question is whether KKLue owns that story loudly enough that it becomes what the brand IS — not a footnote in the About page.

**Priya:** The real question is whether personalization at this level — true made-to-order, up to nine letters, proprietary font — is a product moat or a production liability at scale. I need to understand what happens to that model at 10x volume before I can say it's a differentiator and not a constraint.

**Kellyn:** Our differentiator is the thing we've never compromised on. I have never put a piece into production that I wasn't willing to wear myself. That's not a brand positioning statement — that's how we've run this company for nine years. I don't know how to replicate that with a competitor who doesn't mean it.

**Marcus:** At volume, made-to-order is the differentiator and the risk simultaneously. If KKLue can prove the model holds at 5x demand, that's a story no mass-market brand can tell. If it breaks at 5x, it's not a differentiator anymore.

### Round 4: "What is the single most likely reason this brand fails in Year 1?"

**Why this question:** Asking for kill conditions is different from asking for risks. Everyone has a different one. Together the seven answers form a risk map that covers the full surface area of Year 1 failure modes — customer, brand, ops, competitive, financial, and founder. The point isn't to be pessimistic. The point is to see the map clearly before you start driving.

**The compound scenario the simulation must also run:** What happens when three kill conditions hit simultaneously? Amy's "no US social proof at point of purchase" + Marcus's "18-21 day delivery timeline" + Jade's "inauthenticity detection" = a new US customer finds KKLue, wants to buy, checks for US reviews (finds none), checks delivery time (finds 18-21 days), then looks at the brand Instagram and something feels constructed. She closes the tab. This isn't three separate risks. It's the same customer, in the same five minutes. The simulation should run this scenario explicitly.

**Kill conditions:**

**Jade's kill condition — inauthenticity:** The brand story doesn't hold up under scrutiny. One claim that can't be verified — one misleading product description, one founder quote that feels written by a copywriter, one photoshoot that looks nothing like the actual product — and I'm gone. And I'll say something on Reddit. The trust KKLue needs from a customer like me is extremely fragile at first and it's built one honest detail at a time.

**Amy's kill condition — insufficient social proof at point of purchase:** I find the brand through a TikTok, I go to the website, there are no reviews from US customers, no creators I recognize wearing it, no answers to the specific questions I have (karat, durability, shipping time). I close the tab. The purchase never happens. KKLue needs social proof that's real and accessible at the moment of decision — not after it.

**Vivian's kill condition — brand dilution in chase of mainstream appeal:** KKLue rewrites their story to sound more American and loses the specificity that made it real. The HK origin gets softened into "globally inspired." The personalization story gets simplified into a trendy "self-expression" angle. The founder's backstory gets flattened into a hero journey template. And then there's nothing left that Mejuri couldn't say on its own website.

**Alex's kill condition — too many channels, no depth:** KKLue launches on Instagram, TikTok, XHS, and Pinterest simultaneously in Year 1, spreads the budget across all four, builds no meaningful community on any of them, and runs out of money before organic momentum could have kicked in. We've seen this. It's the most common failure mode in DTC brand building. Depth wins. Not coverage.

**Priya's kill condition — burning Year 1 budget on awareness before product-market fit is confirmed:** KKLue spends $60K on influencer partnerships and brand photography in the first six months, generates some traffic and some first purchases, but has no repeat purchase data, no referral data, and no signal on what's actually driving conversion. Then Year 2 budget conversations happen with no proof. That's how brands end.

**Kellyn's kill condition — running out of money before US brand awareness forms:** The craft story matters. But the real founder kill condition isn't losing the narrative — it's spending Year 1 budget on the wrong things and arriving at Month 12 with no proof of traction, no US customer community, and no clear thesis for Year 2 investment. The craft story only gets told if the brand is still standing to tell it. Burn rate against awareness spend, before product-market fit is confirmed, is how this ends.

**Marcus's kill condition — ops failure during a viral moment, specifically:** A creator post hits 2 million views on a Thursday. By Saturday, the "L" and "K" letter combinations — two of the most-ordered initials — are sold out. You're 21 days from restock on made-to-order. Every new comment on the viral post is "when will this ship?" Customer service can't respond fast enough. The brand's first impression with its most enthusiastic new audience is a three-week wait and silence. That is not recoverable. The brands that die this way never get a second viral moment — because the first one is now a warning, not a recommendation.

### Round 5: "What does success look like at Month 12 — and what single metric would make you double down on this brand?"

**Why this question:** This is the alignment check. Every stakeholder has a different definition of what Year 1 is for — revenue, community, repeat purchase, press, ops proof, brand integrity. The metric each person names reveals what they're actually optimizing for. If the team is not aligned on this before Year 1 starts, they'll disagree about whether Year 1 succeeded even if it went exactly as planned.

**Opening positions:**

**Jade:** At Month 12, I should be able to find 20-30 real customers posting organic content — not sponsored, not gifted, just posting because they love the piece. People showing the necklace on their neck at a birthday dinner. People posting "six months in, still looks new." That's success. My metric: the ratio of organic brand mentions to sponsored posts. If organic is winning, the brand is working.

**Amy:** At Month 12, I want to hear about it from someone in my life. Not a TikTok algorithm. Not an ad. A person. My neighbor, someone in my WeChat group, a colleague who I know has taste. When word of mouth has crossed into real-world networks — that's success. My metric: referral purchase rate. How many buyers came because someone they trusted said go.

**Vivian:** At Month 12, the brand's US content should feel like an extension of what KKLue already is — not a translation of it. Success is KKLue being recognized by HK diaspora women in the US as the brand they knew at home, and being discovered by non-diaspora women as something new and real. Both at the same time. My metric: the ratio of diaspora vs. non-diaspora buyers, and whether both groups are coming back.

**Alex:** At Month 12, KKLue should own a specific, recognizable moment or audience — not be "a jewelry brand for women." Own something specific and be the obvious choice for that specific thing. My metric: category search ranking. When a woman searches "solid gold personalized necklace" on any platform, is KKLue in the conversation? If yes, Year 1 worked. If not, the work isn't done.

**Priya:** At Month 12, I want to see the repeat purchase rate. Not revenue, not follower count, not press hits. What percentage of first-time buyers bought again within six months? If that number is above 25%, the product is real and the brand is working. If it's below 10%, something is broken — either the product isn't what customers expected, or the brand has no retention strategy, or both. That's my number.

**Kellyn:** At Month 12, I want to see letters from customers. Actual messages — a woman who named her necklace, or sent us a photo wearing the piece at her mother's birthday, or bought a second one because the first one meant so much. I can't reduce that to a metric. But I can tell you that if we don't have those stories at Month 12, something went wrong. The jewelry matters to people or it doesn't. That's the only success condition I ultimately care about.

**Marcus:** At Month 12, the ops model should have survived a demand event without a major failure. We should have validated the production capacity ceiling, established a buffer inventory protocol for peak periods, and gotten the US delivery time below 15 days for standard orders. If the brand has grown without a fulfillment crisis, Year 1 ops succeeded. My metric: on-time delivery rate for US orders during the single highest-demand week of the year. If that's above 90%, I'm confident in Year 2.

### Stop Criteria (added after Brutalist review)

**What this round must also produce:** At what point do you stop? If the debate doesn't generate agreed stop criteria, it's a hope document — not a strategy. The simulation must end Round 5 with each stakeholder stating their personal stop condition:

- **Jade:** If by Month 6 I can't find 5 real customers posting organically, I assume the brand didn't earn trust. Not a pivot signal — a stop signal.
- **Priya:** If repeat purchase rate is below 10% at Month 9, the product isn't delivering on the promise. Stop paid spend, go back to product and message testing.
- **Alex:** If KKLue isn't the obvious answer to "solid gold personalized necklace" in at least one platform's search results by Month 12, the channel bet failed. Reassess before Year 2 spend.
- **Marcus:** If a demand spike has occurred and ops failed to deliver above 80% on-time, the made-to-order model needs restructuring before scaling.
- **Kellyn:** If the US content doesn't feel like it came from the same brand that earned 300,000 HK customers, stop and rewrite before spending more.

If KKLue can't state its own stop criteria clearly at the start of Year 1, no one in this room should fund Year 2.

## How to Use This Document

Two modes, depending on audience and moment:

**Mode 1 — Pitch mode.** Present to KKLue as evidence that every angle has been examined before strategy commitments are made. Walk through each persona, show where the tensions live, show how the strategy is designed to resolve the most critical ones. This demonstrates rigor, not just vision.

**Mode 2 — Simulation mode.** Run the full debate as a document — each persona speaks through all five rounds with real dialogue, cross-examination, and resolution. This becomes the social media strategy rationale artifact: a document that shows not just what the strategy is, but why every alternative was considered and set aside. (This is Step C — pending Brutalist review in Step B.)

---

**Step B — Brutalist Review:** A single brutal reader reviews this framework for softness, missing tensions, and questions that aren't hard enough. Any persona that sounds too cooperative or any round that has an obvious right answer gets flagged for strengthening before the simulation runs.

**Step C — Full Simulation:** Each persona speaks through all five rounds with real dialogue. The simulation generates the strategy rationale document. Conflicts that can't be resolved in the room are documented as live tensions that require founder decision.

### A Note on Analogies Used in This Framework

**Mejuri's zero-paid-ads growth** is cited in supporting research as a playbook. Important caveat: Mejuri's early traction was in Toronto, their founder's home city, with physical presence and existing network. They had a home base that generated word-of-mouth before it became a strategy. KKLue has no US home base. The analogy is instructive but not prescriptive — Mejuri's arc is what's possible; it is not a proof of what KKLue will experience.

**Kinn Studio's cultural identity play** worked because Jennie Yoon IS Korean-American — the authenticity was structural, not positioned. Vivian Zhang in this framework is a fictional persona, not KKLue's founder. The Kinn analogy describes a pattern; it does not guarantee the same result for a HK brand whose founder is not a US immigrant.

## What the Room Resolved

**Resolved: Year 1 is a discovery phase, not a growth phase.** The goal is not revenue at scale. The goal is proof — repeat purchase, organic referral, product-market fit signal. Anyone treating Year 1 as a growth year is optimizing for the wrong thing.

**Resolved: Primary metric = repeat purchase rate above 20% by Month 12.** Everything else is secondary. Organic posts, referrals, search ranking, delivery metrics, letters from customers — all real, all tracked, all meaningful. None of them are the primary number. Repeat purchase rate is the only metric that proves both product and brand are working simultaneously.

**Resolved: Trust architecture must exist before marketing.** Google footprint, 750 hallmark documentation, Thingtesting listing, US-accessible reviews — these are pre-launch requirements, not Year 1 goals. A customer who does research at the moment of purchase and finds nothing goes elsewhere. Trust infrastructure is what converts the TikTok view into a tab that stays open.

**Resolved: Channel decision deferred until the customer sentence is finalized.** The working sentence is: "She's a woman who wants one piece of jewelry — her name, her kid's initial, the word she lives by — that she'll still be wearing in ten years, and she's finally ready to pay for something real instead of settling." That sentence has to stop being tentative before channel strategy begins. The channel follows the customer.

**Resolved: Ops protocol for demand spikes must be written before any influencer campaign launches.** Not a crisis plan. A pre-launch checklist: production capacity ceiling, order volume triggers, customer communication protocols, buffer inventory for highest-demand letter combinations, on-time delivery targets. Written. Owned. Not improvised.

---

**Unresolved: Is the target customer "burned by fake gold" or "already knows what real gold is"?**

These are different women. The burned customer needs reassurance — she needs proof that this isn't the necklace that turned green. The discerning customer doesn't need to be reassured — she just needs to be shown something she hasn't seen before. Different messaging. Different creative. Different acquisition channels. The room couldn't decide which woman to reach first, because reaching both simultaneously splits the message and dilutes both.

Recommendation: don't decide in a room. Split-test it. First 10 gifted creators, 5 per message angle. Five creators leading with the proof story ("18K solid gold, worn for six months, here's how it's held up"). Five creators leading with the discovery story ("I wasn't looking for a new jewelry brand — and then I found this"). Let the data tell you which customer converts, which customer comes back, which customer refers. That decision deserves real signal, not a room's best guess.

**Unresolved: City beachhead versus online-first.**

Several voices in the room implied a geographic concentration strategy — LA, NYC, the diaspora-dense markets. The counterargument is that online-first keeps options open: let the first 100 orders tell you where she actually lives before you double down on a market. Billing zip codes from the first 100 customers are more honest than any targeting assumption. Recommendation: launch online-first, track billing zip codes, and let the data build the geographic thesis. If orders cluster — a specific metro, a specific zip code density — that's where you double down in Year 2. Don't assume the beachhead. Find it.
