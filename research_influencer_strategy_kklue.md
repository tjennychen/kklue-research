## 1. Strategic Positioning for Influencer Marketing

### The Story KKlue Tells Through Influencers

Not: "beautiful jewelry from Hong Kong."
Not: "sustainable luxury for the modern woman."

The actual story: **"You got burned. So did she."**

Every woman KKlue is targeting has bought a gold-plated necklace that turned green. Monica Vinader. Missoma. Gorjana. Mejuri's vermeil line. She spent $80-200 on something that looked great for six weeks and now sits in a drawer. She's angry about it, she talks about it on Reddit and TikTok, and she's ready to buy something that won't lie to her about what it is.

KKlue's influencer content is the answer to that anger. Not condescending — just accurate. 18K solid gold at $300 costs the same as what she's been buying. It won't tarnish. She can shower in it. That's the story. Everything else (HK craft origin, made-to-order, brand story) is the texture that gives it credibility.

### The 3 Content Angles That Will Work

**Angle 1: "I've worn it every day for [X] months."**
The single most powerful format. Matches KKlue's best-performing XHS content verbatim ("365天戴300天以上"). A creator who received a gifted piece in Month 1 and posts a follow-up in Month 4 still wearing it is worth 10 product launch posts. Brief for this explicitly. It answers the only question that matters at $300+: "Is it actually worth it?"

**Angle 2: Gold education without naming names.**
"Here's what's in your jewelry drawer and why it turned green" content. This is a blue ocean category — none of the 13 competitors researched are running jewelry material education as a creator content category. The hook: show what 18K means (the 750 hallmark stamp), explain vermeil vs. solid, demonstrate the difference. Creators who do "invest in quality" content organically are the right fit. This content has very high save rates (purchase intent signal) and ages well on Pinterest.

**Angle 3: Milestone personalization reveal.**
Custom letters, names, a word that means something. Opening a made-to-order piece in 18K gold with your kids' initials or your own name hits a specific emotional register. GLDN has proven this format drives direct purchase intent at their price point. KKlue's version is more premium and the customization is deeper (up to 9 letters, brand font). This is the format for Mother's Day, birthday, and self-gifting campaigns. Brief creators to tie it to a real personal moment — not "here's a cute necklace" but "I ordered this with my daughter's name after she turned 5."

The narrative-driven version of this angle is where virality lives. @darylanndenner (Ring Concierge program) ran a story-driven giveaway with a real emotional hook — "engagement ring fell in the lake" — and got 528K likes + 1.009M comments on one post. The best engagement in Ring Concierge's entire program came from a narrative-first format, not a product announcement. KKlue's equivalent: "I've worn this necklace every day since my daughter was born" or "I ordered this with my grandma's name the week she passed." The milestone personalization angle told through a real story is the format to brief for.

Cultural specificity, not demographic breadth, is where the ROI lives.

Additionally: Korean-American and Chinese-American creators are already more receptive to the material education angle because K-gold literacy exists in that community. They know what 18K means. The gap to bridge is smaller. And once they post, their audiences are also more likely to be pre-educated on material quality — shortening the purchase consideration cycle.

**The defensibility:** By the time Ring Concierge or Mejuri notices KKlue, the brand will have authentic creator relationships in a niche that would require years of genuine cultural integration for them to replicate credibly. KKlue owns it by birthright.

### "Real Gold vs. Plated" Education — How to Execute Without Naming Competitors

Never mention Mejuri, Missoma, Gorjana, or any competitor by name. Instead:

- Brief creators to talk about their own past experience: "the necklace that turned green," "I thought I was buying gold," "the third one I've owned that's done this" — real stories from their own jewelry drawer
- Give creators the factual language: 18K solid gold = 750 hallmark = 75% pure gold. Gold vermeil = sterling silver base with thin gold coating. Gold-plated = base metal with even thinner gold coating. These are just definitions, not attacks.
- The Polene / Tanner Leatherstein angle: a jewelry educator or goldsmith creator (not a fashion influencer) showing the hallmark on a KKlue piece, explaining what it means, weighing the piece in their hand — this is the credibility validator format. It compounds. Creators who post "comparison" or "is it worth it" content in the jewelry space have built-in audiences looking for this information.
- Let creators' audiences do the comparative work in the comments. They will. Don't script it.

## 2. Tier Strategy for KKlue Year 1

### Recommended Split: Micro-Only, Nano-Seeding, No Paid Fees in Year 1

**Budget reality (from GTM plan):**
- Q1 gifting: $6,000-8,000 at ~$150-200/unit cost = 30-40 pieces
- Q2 gifting: $3,000-5,000 = 10-15 additional pieces
- Year 1 influencer total: ~$9,000-13,000 — product cost only, no cash fees

| Tier | Followers | Role | Year 1 Volume | Cost Model |
|------|-----------|------|---------------|------------|
| Nano | 1K-10K | Seeding, XHS diaspora creators, early community | 30-40 in Q1 | Gifting only (~$150-200/piece at cost) |
| Micro | 30K-150K | Primary conversion engine — the Year 1 workhorse | 10-15 in Q2-Q4 | Gifting only; affiliate code for proven posters |
| Mid-tier | 150K-500K | Skip Year 1 — budget doesn't support even gifting at retail price | 0 | N/A |
| Macro/Mega | 500K+ | Skip Year 1 entirely | 0 | N/A |

**The sweet spot for KKlue in Year 1:** 30K-150K followers. Within budget (product cost only), personal enough audience for $300 recommendations to land with trust, and the tier that drove early growth for Polene, Mejuri Fine Crew, and Monica Vinader Insiders. No cash fees in Year 1 — gifting + optional affiliate code (10-15% commission) for creators who post organically.

### Why KKlue Should NOT Copy Ring Concierge's Macro-First Approach

Ring Concierge's first co-created product was with Coco Schiffer (macro). Their paid partnerships are all macro tier — Rocky Barnes (3.3M), Matilda Djerf (3M), Andi Dorfman (1M).

This works for Ring Concierge because:
1. The founder, Nicole Wegman, has a personal Instagram following that is itself macro-scale. She IS the micro-influencer at scale.
2. Ring Concierge entered the market before iOS 14 privacy changes made organic reach predictable.
3. They were not entering cold — they built audience first through the founder account for years before macro partnerships.

KKlue has zero US brand recognition. A macro partnership with a creator who has 1M+ followers whose audience has never heard of KKlue produces a blip and moves on. No compounding. High cost. No community infrastructure to receive it. Worse: it signals budget over authenticity, which is the opposite of KKlue's positioning.

The better comparison: Polene grew to €142M in revenue entirely through micro/nano seeding. 8,700+ creators mentioned the brand. The brand's visual distinctiveness meant every post added to an ambient social proof pile. KKlue's 18K gold + personalization story is equally distinctive. The Polene model fits.

### Why KKlue SHOULD Start with Polene-Style Seeding + GLDN-Style Milestone Storytelling

**Polene model:** Send product to 20-30 creators with no posting obligation. Genuine invitation, not a transaction. Track who posts organically. Follow up with those who do. Convert the best organic posters to paid partnerships. This produces authentic content that converts better than scripted paid posts — and the budget for 25-30 gifted pieces at product cost (~$7,500) is the lowest cost creative production in marketing.

**GLDN model:** Brief creators to tie the piece to a real personal milestone. GLDN's entire content program is built around "this piece means something to me" — an engagement, a new baby, a name necklace for a lost parent. This is KKlue's natural content territory. The Moment necklace is literally made to carry names. The emotional connection content drives purchase intent because it reframes the $300 price as a memory investment, not a jewelry purchase.

Combined: seed broadly in the Polene style, brief specifically in the GLDN milestone style for creators who respond. Add ShopMy as the affiliate layer from Day 1 — it gives creators who post organically a reason to keep posting after the gifted period ends.

The competitor data also confirms this model is structurally correct:

| Brand | Paid median likes | Affiliate median likes |
|---|---|---|
| GLDN | 60 | 122 |
| Linjer | 188 | 407 |
| Gorjana | 370 | 254 |
| Alohas | 128 | 394 |
| Monica Vinader | 299 | 270 |
| Mejuri | 530 | 596 |

Content that looks organic converts better than content that looks paid — across every brand tested. Gifting-first is not just budget-efficient. It's the right structure.

One more archetype to recruit for the LTK/ShopMy layer: **The "maybeboth" archetype** — @maybeboth in Mejuri's program. LTK lifestyle creator, not a jewelry specialist. 12 posts, 500,974 total likes = 14.1% of Mejuri's entire 2,000-post program. She's someone whose audience trusts her product taste broadly. KKlue's version: an AAPI lifestyle creator on ShopMy who happens to wear KKlue every day and builds a purchase history with the brand. Not a jewelry creator. A lifestyle creator who wears it.

## 3. Influencer Niches to Target (Priority Ordered)

### Priority 1: AAPI Fashion / Lifestyle Creators (US-Based)

**Why it fits KKlue:** Authentic brand origin. Real cultural connection to gold as investment. Pre-existing literacy on material quality. Community recommendation structure (AAPI women trust peer recommendations within the community — XHS is evidence of this working at scale in Chinese). No competitor has this relationship.

**Target follower range:** 20K-150K. This tier is active, personal, and affordable to gift.

**Creator archetypes:**
- Second-generation Chinese-American or Korean-American woman, late 20s-mid 30s, professional career or self-employed, posts about fashion/lifestyle/daily life on Instagram, also on XHS or YouTube. She's not a "fashion influencer" — she's a person who happens to have an audience. She references quality products she keeps. She's been burned by tarnishing jewelry and has said so publicly.
- Hong Kong diaspora creator: post-2019 emigrant, nostalgia for HK brands, bridge between HK culture and US life. Very specific fit for KKlue's brand origin.
- AAPI entrepreneur or creative professional who posts about building her career, her aesthetic, her life — jewelry is incidental to the content but worn consistently in photos.

**Named creator archetypes from competitor data (real examples to recruit against):**

- **The "chanelogy" archetype** (organic cultural fit) — @chanelogy in Senreve's program. Asian-American, pet + fashion content. 5 posts, top post = 6,939 likes. No formal ambassador structure. The best AAPI-signal performer in Senreve's entire program and she wasn't even a formal partner. When cultural fit is real, it outperforms structured paid deals. Recruit 8-12 of this archetype for Year 1.

- **The "on_se_voit_a_stutzheim" archetype** (cultural bridge creator) — @on_se_voit_a_stutzheim in Linjer's program. Cantonese lifestyle creator, writes long-form captions about Norwegian minimalist identity in Cantonese. 5 posts, 32,155 total likes. Most efficient creator in Linjer's 508-person roster. Proves that cultural specificity, not broad AAPI targeting, is where disproportionate engagement lives. KKlue is the reverse: a HK origin brand being introduced to the US through Korean-American and Chinese-American creators.

- **The "sanjanaagutha" archetype** (travel + aesthetic, genuinely organic) — @sanjanaagutha in Senreve's program. Travel/lifestyle creator. 6 posts, all "Unspecified" (no formal deal). 10K, 5K, 4K, 2K likes across posts. Appears to be genuine brand love, not a structured partnership. KKlue equivalent: an Asian-American travel or lifestyle creator who wears the necklace to dinners and work trips, not a dedicated jewelry post.

**Expected content type:** Lifestyle integration (jewelry in the frame across many posts), wear test follow-ups, gift reveal at seasonal moments (CNY, Mother's Day).

**Estimated cost:**
- Gifting only: product cost ~$300/piece
- Gifted + affiliate (10-15% commission): product cost + $0 upfront cash
- Paid post for proven organic posters: $150-400/post (micro range)

**Skeptic's note:** AAPI creators with 20K-80K followers who are genuinely a fit are not as easy to find as it sounds. Modash's demographic filters are a proxy, not a guarantee. The real filter is manual: read their captions, look at what other brands they wear, check if they've posted about jewelry quality before. Plan for 2-3 hours of vetting per creator, not 10 minutes.

### Priority 2: Professional Women / Career Milestone Creators

**Why it fits KKlue:** Senreve (bag brand, same customer) proved this niche converts for considered-purchase quality products. No jewelry brand in the research set owns "invest in yourself, career milestone" messaging. The Moment necklace at $300 positioned as a self-gift after a promotion or first year of running your own business is a completely natural purchase. The "personalized letters" angle is especially strong here — your initials, your company initials, a word you're claiming.

**Target follower range:** 15K-100K. This niche doesn't require huge follower counts — a career coach or entrepreneur with 25K highly engaged professional-women followers converts better than a general lifestyle account with 200K.

**Creator archetypes:**
- Women entrepreneurship/career content creator: posts about building a business, negotiating salary, navigating corporate, "what I've learned" style content. Jewelry worn incidentally in professional settings.
- "Treat yourself after the milestone" content: women who document promotions, business launches, work anniversaries — and buy quality things to mark the moment.
- Working mom who self-gifts: posts about balancing career and family, wears jewelry daily as personal expression. The combination of her kids' initials on a necklace she wears every day is a natural purchase story.

**Expected content type:** "Why I bought this" narrative post, milestone reveal, styling in professional contexts (the necklace visible in a Zoom meeting, in a conference, in a morning commute photo).

**Estimated cost:** Same as Priority 1. Gifting-first, convert best performers to paid.

**Skeptic's note:** This niche can drift into generic "girl boss" content that reads as inauthentic. The filter: does this creator actually wear fine jewelry, or just aspirational lifestyle content? Check their existing jewelry posts. If they're wearing Gorjana and complaining about tarnishing, that's the warm lead. If they've never posted about jewelry before, the gifted piece will look awkward in their content.

### Priority 3: Quality Education / "Worth It" Fashion Investment Creators

**Why it fits KKlue:** This is the Tanner Leatherstein opportunity for jewelry. There is no widely-known jewelry equivalent of Tanner — someone who evaluates material quality credibly on video, has an audience that trusts them on "is this actually good," and can demonstrate what 18K solid gold actually means vs. gold plating. This content is blue ocean in the US jewelry creator space.

**Target follower range:** 10K-150K. This is a niche with high engagement even at smaller follower counts because the audience is highly intent-driven.

**Creator archetypes:**
- "Slow fashion / buy less, buy better" lifestyle creator: references investment pieces, talks about cost per wear, posts capsule wardrobe content. Her audience trusts her on quality assessments.
- Jewelry educator or goldsmith on YouTube/TikTok: already posts about gold karats, hallmarking, the difference between gold-filled and solid gold. Small but highly qualified audience.
- "What I actually own and love after 2 years" type creator: posts thoughtful retrospectives on purchases, with honest assessments. High saved content rate.

**Expected content type:** Durability evidence post ("still looks new after 6 months"), material comparison content ("here's the hallmark, here's what it means"), cost-per-wear breakdown.

**Estimated cost:** Gifting. These creators are often not running high-volume paid partnership programs because they're selective about what they endorse. The gifted piece has to genuinely earn the endorsement.

**Skeptic's note:** Don't manufacture this content. If you brief a lifestyle creator who's never talked about material quality to suddenly become a jewelry educator, it reads as sponsored content pretending to be organic education. The authentic version requires finding creators who already talk this way and whose natural content happens to be a perfect home for the KKlue material story.

### Priority 4: Sustainable Luxury / Real Materials / Investment Pieces

**Why it fits KKlue:** "Real gold IS the original sustainable material" is an angle nobody has owned in this space. Linjer leads with recycled gold + ethical sourcing. Aurate leads with B Corp. GLDN leads with recycled metals. They're all framing sustainability as ethics. The frame nobody is using: solid gold doesn't end up in a landfill. It doesn't tarnish. You don't replace it. You pass it down. That's sustainability through material permanence, not sourcing certification.

**Target follower range:** 20K-100K. Slow fashion community on Instagram has deep engagement.

**Creator archetypes:**
- Slow fashion / capsule wardrobe creator: already talks about investment pieces, posts "what's still in my wardrobe after 3 years" content, anti-fast fashion messaging.
- Minimal aesthetic lifestyle creator who references "quality over quantity" regularly.
- Creator who has already made the XHS-equivalent of "I wear this 300 days a year" type posts about other products.

**Expected content type:** "Investment piece" roundup, cost-per-wear analysis, "what I've kept in my jewelry collection," minimal fashion lookbook where the KKlue piece features prominently.

**Estimated cost:** Gifting + affiliate. This niche responds well to affiliate because they actively recommend products to audiences who ask "where is that from?"

**Skeptic's note:** "Sustainable luxury" is cluttered messaging territory and the audience is sophisticated enough to see through shallow versions. KKlue's GRS-certified SMILEY collection is real, but it's not the hero product. Don't lead with sustainability claims on the Moment necklace. Lead with material permanence ("this gold doesn't end") and let sustainability be the subtext.

### Priority 5: Gen X Women (35-50) — Underserved Demographic

**Why it fits KKlue:** The entire competitor set — Mejuri, Gorjana, Adina Eden, Ring Concierge — skews millennial and Gen Z in their creator partnerships. Nobody is explicitly targeting the 35-50 woman with more purchasing power, less brand loyalty to the incumbents, and a completely different purchasing psychology. She doesn't self-gift the way a 28-year-old does. She buys for permanence. She's done experimenting with fashion jewelry. She wants one piece she wears every day for the next 10 years.

KKlue's solid 18K at $300 is exactly the proposition for this woman.

**Target follower range:** 15K-80K. This demographic over-indexes on Instagram relative to TikTok.

**Creator archetypes:**
- Women 40+ lifestyle creator who posts about midlife self-reinvention, professional evolution, "things I've stopped buying" content — classic "worth it" buyer.
- Entrepreneur/business owner in 40s who posts about building her career, her aesthetic, and her life. Disposable income, high quality standards, skeptical of hype.
- Mom of teens or young adults who is post-"mom identity" and reclaiming personal style.

**Expected content type:** "Why I finally bought real jewelry" narrative, jewelry as self-permission ("I spent 20 years buying $50 necklaces; this is what I should have done"), wear test over months.

**Estimated cost:** Gifting-first. This tier is not running volume paid campaigns — they're selective. The gifted piece has to earn the post.

**Skeptic's note:** Gen X creators with 15K-80K followers are genuinely harder to find in Modash's filters, which skew toward younger fashion content. Manual discovery via hashtag search and competitor-follower analysis is required. Also: this demographic has a longer purchase consideration cycle. Don't expect a gifted piece to result in a post within 2 weeks. The 3-month follow-up ("still wearing it, still perfect") is the format.

### Priority 6: Diaspora Gift-Giving (Chinese/AAPI Cultural Moments)

**Why it fits KKlue:** Chinese New Year, mid-autumn festival, graduations (a massive gift moment in Chinese families), weddings — gold is culturally embedded as the right gift in these moments. No US jewelry brand is building content around CNY gift-giving. KKlue can own this seasonal content category by default.

**Target follower range:** 10K-60K. XHS-active creators who post across platforms, or AAPI lifestyle creators who celebrate these cultural moments publicly.

**Creator archetypes:**
- AAPI lifestyle creator who posts about cultural holidays — CNY celebrations, family dinners, gifting traditions. This is a natural content home for "I got this gold necklace as a CNY gift from my mom."
- First-gen or 1.5-gen Chinese-American creator who bridges both cultures — posts in English but celebrates Chinese cultural moments. This is KKlue's brand story made human.

**Expected content type:** Gift reveal (unboxing a KKlue piece as a gift), "what I'm getting my mom for CNY," personalized necklace with family initials as heritage piece.

**Estimated cost:** Gifting. Time gifting outreach 4-6 weeks before cultural holidays.

**Skeptic's note:** Don't only activate this niche around cultural moments or it reads as performative. These creators need to be genuine KKlue fans year-round who happen to also post around cultural moments. The cultural moment post has higher reach, but the authentic relationship has to exist before it.

### Month 2-3: Outreach Template

**DM template (personalized per creator — the bracket text must be customized):**

> Hi [Name] — I've been following your content for a while and [specific reference to a real post of theirs, e.g., "loved your post about the Polene bag you've had for 3 years"]. I run KKlue, a Hong Kong fine jewelry brand — we make personalized letter necklaces in 18K solid gold, made to order.
>
> I'd love to send you the Moment necklace with [suggest their initials or something specific from their content] — no obligation to post, I genuinely just want you to wear it and see what you think. The piece is solid 18K gold; shower in it, sleep in it, it won't change.
>
> If you're open to it, just reply with your address and I'll send it over.

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't send a press kit as the first contact
- Don't include "we'd love to collaborate" language in the first message — it reads as transactional
- Don't ask for a post, a story, or any content commitment in first contact
- Don't CC a PR email address — this signals the DM is automated
- Don't send to 50 people the same week with the same message — response rates tank when creators see templated outreach

**Email follow-up (if no DM response in 5 days):**
Find creator email via Modash. Send a brief version of the above. Keep it under 5 sentences. Attach one product photo — the Moment necklace on model, not a white background shot.

- Commission: 15% on affiliate link sales (already running from Phase 1 — keep it)

### Co-Design Partnership Playbook (Missoma Model Adapted for KKlue)

**When:** Month 9 at earliest, only if the right creator has emerged from Phase 1-2.

**The Missoma proof point:** @lucywilliams02's co-designed Roman Arc necklace sold 50,000+ units. Her 3-piece "Entwine Edit" drove 40% of all Missoma online sales in July 2024. Started as gifting → became affiliate (personal code LUCY30) → became a co-designed product line. 45 posts across 3+ years. That compounding required the relationship to start in Year 1. Co-design is the highest-ROI influencer model in this research set.

**KKlue's version:**

The natural co-design product for KKlue is a Moment necklace variation or a limited-edition pendant design bearing the creator's aesthetic signature. The KKlue proprietary Brand Font is a canvas — a creator could have input on pendant shape, letter style variation, a special charm, or a piece that reflects their personal story.

**Who to target for co-design:**

- AAPI creator, 100K-300K Instagram followers
- Has posted about KKlue organically in Phase 1-2 (authentic connection is mandatory — a paid co-design with someone who's never engaged with the brand will look exactly like what it is)
- Has an identifiable personal aesthetic that can translate to jewelry design
- Her audience is 25-40, US-based, professional women or AAPI community
- She has some cultural connection to the KKlue brand story (HK origin, immigrant journey, real gold as heirloom)

**Archetype:** Second-generation Chinese-American or AAPI woman with 150K-300K Instagram followers, runs lifestyle/fashion content with authentic professional voice, is known for having taste and opinions — not just posting pretty photos. She's someone her audience asks "where did you get that." She might be a designer, architect, interior stylist, or creative entrepreneur herself.

**How to structure the deal:**
- 6-month partnership minimum
- Co-design involvement: 2-3 creative sessions (can be remote), final approval on the piece
- Revenue share: 10-15% on sales of the co-designed piece, in addition to flat monthly fee
- Exclusivity in fine jewelry category (not all jewelry)
- Content requirement: 2 posts/month for the duration of the partnership + co-design reveal campaign (4-6 posts)
- Minimum 6 months of authentic brand relationship before the co-design conversation begins

**Deal size expectation:** $1,500-3,000/month flat fee + 10-15% commission on co-designed piece sales. For a successful co-design with 50-100 pieces sold in the first month, this can generate $1,500-4,500 in commission on top of the flat fee.

## 6. What NOT to Do (Anti-Patterns)

### Don't chase celebrity gifting cold

Bychari's celebrity placement (Michelle Obama, Kate Hudson) was the result of years of systematic stylist seeding in LA. It looked organic because it was. The celebrity moment can't be engineered, but the stylist relationship network that positioned the brand to receive it can. For KKlue in Year 1: build a US stylist/editorial gifting list of 20-30 contacts as a slow background task — not for immediate payoff but as infrastructure. Don't send to celebrity agents or celebrity DMs directly. It reads as amateur and almost never lands.

### Don't launch a mass open affiliate program before brand recognition

Commission-only programs require brand gravity to work. Mejuri's Fine Crew works because creators want to be associated with Mejuri. Monica Vinader's 16K-member Insiders program works because MV is aspirational. KKlue with zero US brand recognition launching an open "sign up to be our affiliate" program will attract the wrong tier of creator (anyone who monetizes every small DTC brand that reaches out) and produce content that looks transactional, not genuine. Earn the organic posts first. Build the affiliate program in Year 2 when you have something to offer beyond commission.

### Don't position around sustainability first

Linjer leads with recycled gold + Scandinavian ethics. Aurate leads with B Corp. GLDN leads with recycled metals. Everyone in this space claims sustainability. It's not a differentiator — it's expected. KKlue's SMILEY x KKlue collection IS genuinely sustainability-certified (GRS + FSC), but leading with sustainability messaging when entering a market where multiple competitors have established sustainability positioning is fighting on crowded ground. Sustainability is the supporting story for the brand; the material truth (18K solid gold doesn't tarnish and doesn't end up in landfill) is the headline.

### Don't try to be Mejuri

Mejuri owns "self-gifting DTC minimalism for the modern woman." Their hashtag community has hundreds of thousands of posts. Their aesthetic is so established that any brand that looks like Mejuri reads as a Mejuri knockoff. KKlue's differentiation is in three directions Mejuri can't go: HK craft origin (authentic, not manufactured), deeper personalization (9 letters, brand font, made to order — not just stamping an initial), and material honesty (18K solid gold across the range, vs. Mejuri's mix of solid and vermeil that customers frequently discover the hard way). Don't chase Mejuri's aesthetic or their creator profile. Find the whitespace.

### Don't start TikTok hard in Year 1

TikTok is excellent for discovery and comparison content. But TikTok reach is geographically unpredictable, TikTok does not close $300 jewelry purchases the way Instagram does, and TikTok's viral moments don't build brand loyalty — they build fleeting awareness. Fine jewelry is not TikTok-native content. Instagram still drives 70%+ of DTC jewelry revenue (documented for Ring Concierge, likely true across the category). Seed 5-10 TikTok creators in Month 4-6 as a secondary experiment. Don't invest cash in TikTok until Instagram has produced real conversion signal.

### Don't brief for OOTD content as the primary deliverable

Stacking and layering jewelry OOTD content is the most saturated format in the jewelry creator space. Mejuri runs it. Adina Eden runs it. Gorjana runs it. For a new brand with no visual recognition, one more layered-jewelry OOTD post does not get noticed. The KKlue differentiators — material quality story, personalization depth, HK craft origin — require more than a wrist shot. Brief for real-life integration and wear tests, not fashion photos.

### Don't equate post volume with success in Year 1

20 influencer posts that have no measurable click-through or conversion signal are worse than 5 posts that drove real affiliate traffic. Optimize for conversion signal, not volume. The post from a 40K-follower creator that drove 30 affiliate link clicks and 3 sales is worth more than 4 posts from macro creators that drove zero attributed revenue.

## 9. KPIs and Measurement

### 30/60/90 Day Checkpoints

**Day 30 (end of Month 1):**
- [ ] Seeding list of 25-30 creators finalized in Modash
- [ ] 15-20 outreach DMs/emails sent
- [ ] 5-8 confirmed gifting recipients (address confirmed, piece ordered)
- [ ] Attribution stack live: promo codes per creator + UTM links set up in Modash + Shopify integration active
- [ ] @kklue_jewelry has 10+ English-language posts live (product + material story)

**Day 60 (end of Month 2):**
- [ ] 25-30 pieces shipped
- [ ] 3-5 organic posts received (if below 3, diagnose creator selection or brief friction)
- [ ] First affiliate link clicks tracked (any is good; track source)
- [ ] At least 2 creator conversations active (DM exchanges, engagement with their posts)
- [ ] Outreach response rate: target 30%+ will respond to the initial DM (will engage even if not all confirm gifting)

**Day 90 (end of Month 3):**
- [ ] 5-8 organic posts from Phase 1 seeding
- [ ] 2-4 creators identified as "champions" (posted, clicked through, actively engaging with brand)
- [ ] Affiliate link + promo code attribution data showing which creators drive traffic (even if no purchases yet)
- [ ] Content library: 5+ pieces of usable UGC for future paid amplification
- [ ] First US English reviews on site (from gifted creators or their buyers)

### What to Measure (Not Just Reach)

| Metric | Why It Matters | Good Signal | Concern |
|--------|---------------|-------------|---------|
| Post rate from gifting | Validates creator fit + product quality | 20-30% | Below 15% |
| Save rate on product posts | Purchase consideration signal | 1%+ on jewelry post | Below 0.5% |
| Affiliate link click-through | Real intent, not just impressions | 2-5% of post reach | Below 1% |
| Promo code redemptions | Direct attribution | 1-3% of link clicks | 0 redemptions at Month 3 |
| DM volume to brand after creator posts | Warm inbound interest | Any increase | No movement |
| Creator re-engagement rate | Relationship quality | Creator posts about brand again without prompting | No organic follow-up |
| Branded search volume lift | Awareness building | Month 3 branded searches higher than Month 1 | Flat |
| US English review count | Trust infrastructure | 5+ by Month 3 | 0 at Month 3 |

**What save rate tells you:** A creator post with 50K reach and 500 saves (1%) is far more valuable than a post with 50K reach and 50 saves (0.1%). Saves signal "I want to come back to this / buy this." For a $300 product requiring consideration, saves predict purchase better than likes.

### When to Pivot if It's Not Working

**If post rate is below 15% by Month 3:** The creator selection is wrong or the brief is creating friction. Run two diagnoses: (1) are the creators actually wearing this type of jewelry in their organic content? (2) Did anything in the outreach process create obligation-feeling pressure? Fix selection before spending more on gifting.

**If affiliate link clicks are below 50 total by Month 3:** The content being produced isn't generating purchase intent. The issue is usually that the content is aesthetic (looks pretty in a photo) but doesn't tell the material story or include a reason to click. Add a clearer brief for Month 4 outreach — specifically ask creators to mention the 18K solid gold if they post.

**If 0 English reviews by Month 3:** The gifted pieces haven't produced public response yet. Direct ask: DM the 3-5 creators who received pieces and ask "if you love it, would you leave a review on the site?" This is a separate ask from the post — reviews are for the website, posts are for their audience.

**If no creator has posted by Month 4:** This is a significant signal. Either the product experience (packaging, quality of product, the unboxing) didn't meet expectations, or the outreach pool was too low-engagement to care. Both are fixable. Get feedback directly from 2-3 creators who received pieces and didn't post — DM and ask honestly "did the piece meet your expectations?"

## Skeptic's Core Point

Every brand looks like it has a "real influencer program." Strip the top 5 posts from any of them and what remains is a lot of low-engagement gifting noise with median 100-500 likes per post. The programs that actually built brand equity -- Missoma's Lucy Williams collab, Linjer's HK cluster, Polene's Korean macro activation -- did it through cultural resonance, not volume. KKlue has a stronger cultural resonance claim than any of them for the AAPI market. The question is whether it commits to building the program structurally or treats AAPI as an occasional heritage month activation (Senreve's mistake). A focused 20-30 creator program with second-gen Korean-American and Chinese-American fashion/lifestyle creators, running clean affiliate, posting milestone content, and building toward one named collab in Year 2, would likely outperform the entire non-athlete Gorjana program, the entire non-Kerry-Washington Aurate program, and the entire non-Aly-AJ GLDN program on engagement per dollar.
